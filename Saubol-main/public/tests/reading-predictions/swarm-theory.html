Reading Passage 3

Swarm theory

I used to think that ants knew what they were doing. The ones marching across my kitchen bench looked so confident that I figured they had a plan, knew where going and what needed to be done. How else could ants organise highways, build elaborate nests, stage epic raids and do all of the other things ants do? But it turns out I was wrong. Ants aren’t clever little engineers… at least not as individuals. When it comes to deciding what to do next, most ants don’t have a clue. ‘If you watch an ant trying to accomplish something, you’ll be impressed by how inept it is,’ says biologist Deborah M. Gordon. How do we explain, then, the success of Earth’s 12,000 or so known ant species?

‘Ants aren’t smart,’ Gordon says. ‘Ant colonies are.’ A colony can solve problems unthinkable to individual ants—finding the shortest path to food, allocating workers to different tasks, defending territory. As individuals, ants might be tiny dummies, but as colonies they respond quickly and effectively to their environment. They do this with something called swarm intelligence Where this intelligence comes from raises a fundamental question in nature: how do the simple actions of individuals add up to the complex behaviour of a group?

Job allocation. In the Arizona desert, where Gordon studies red harvester ants, a colony decides each morning how many workers to send out foraging. The number changes with conditions. An ant may be a nest worker one day, a trash collector the next. But how do they adjust if no one’s in charge?

Ants communicate by touch and smell. When one ant bumps into another, it sniffs with its antennae to find out if the other belongs to the same nest and where it has been working. Before they leave the nest each day, foragers normally wait for early-morning patrollers to return. As patrollers enter, they touch antennae briefly with foragers. To test this, Gordon’s team captured patrollers as they left a nest, waited half an hour, then simulated the ants’ return by dropping glass beads into the entrance—some coated with patroller scent, some with other scents, some with none. Only beads with patroller scent stimulated foragers to leave. If contacts with patrollers occur at the right rate, it’s time to forage; if not, it might be too windy—or a hungry lizard might be outside.

Honeybees and decisions.Thomas Seeley at Cornell studies how swarms choose new homes. His team paint-marked bees and added tiny tags, ferried swarms to Appledore Island, released them to find five test nest boxes, then watched. Scouts danced to advertise locations; the strength of a dance reflected enthusiasm. Once about fifteen scouts gathered at a box, bees sensed a decision had been reached and returned to the swarm with the news. Seeley now applies the bees’ rules—seek diverse opinions, encourage free competition among ideas, and use mechanisms to narrow choices—in his own department.
